How to use this tool

A guide to using the detection method comparison tool.

1. Select Data Source

TrendWatch offers two distinct ways to generate analysis, depending on your needs:

  • Simulation (Generated Data): Select "Simulation" to have the tool automatically generate random numbers based on statistical parameters. This is ideal for theoretical testing where you want to see how a chart reacts to specific distributions (Normal, Lognormal) without needing an external file.
  • Real-World Data (CSV Upload): Select "Upload CSV" to import your own facility's historical data. The tool will calculate the baseline (mean and standard deviation) directly from your file, allowing you to validate methods against actual records.

2. Define Behavior (Simulation Only)

If you chose Simulation, you must define how the data behaves before any anomalies occur:

  • Stable: Data fluctuates around a constant mean (Normal or Lognormal distribution).
  • Trending: Data steadily increases or decreases over time (Linear).
  • Periodic: Data follows a sinusoidal wave pattern.

3. Induce Changes

Force the process to go "Out of Control" on a specific day to test detection speed:

  • Step Change: A sudden instantaneous jump. (e.g., Factor 1.2 = 20% increase).
  • Trending Change: A gradual drift that starts on the change day.
  • Amplitude Change: (Periodic only) The wave height increases/decreases.
  • Period Change: (Periodic only) The cycle frequency speeds up or slows down.

4. Analysis Methods

Shewhart Standard 3-sigma limits. Best for large, sudden spikes.
EWMA Exponentially Weighted Moving Average. Best for small, subtle shifts.
CUSUM Cumulative Sum. Extremely sensitive to small deviations from target.
Farrington / GLM Advanced algorithms for surveillance data.

5. Compare & Finalize

  • Reanalyze: After a run, click "Reanalyze" in the top menu to test a different method (e.g., switching from Shewhart to CUSUM) on the exact same dataset.
  • Finalize & Rank: Click the "Finalize" button to generate a performance table. This ranks your runs by Average Run Length (ARL)—a lower ARL means the method detected the outbreak faster.
